## 2. North Star

High contrast design is not simply "black background, white text." It is a discipline of separation:

- foreground separates from background instantly
- primary actions separate from secondary actions
- brand attitude separates from generic UI
- information hierarchy separates from decoration
- negative space separates content blocks
- one accent separates important moments from the rest

The strongest high-contrast designs feel inevitable. They look like there were fewer possible decisions, not more.

### 2.1 Primary Feeling

Use high contrast when the interface should feel:

- direct
- confident
- editorial
- technical
- premium
- cinematic
- poster-like
- precise
- confrontational in a controlled way
- legible at a glance

Avoid high contrast when the product needs:

- softness as the dominant emotion
- long relaxed reading sessions without tonal shifts
- dense dashboards without careful secondary tones
- childlike warmth
- low-arousal wellness tone
- decorative illustration as the main identity system

## 3. Style Archetypes

### 3.1 Theatrical Black Canvas

Inspired by Colab.

This archetype uses pure or near-pure black as a stage. White type is the actor. A single accent color becomes a lighting cue.

Best for:

- creative agencies
- artist portfolios
- cultural projects
- premium launch pages
- campaign sites
- brand microsites

Core traits:

- black full-bleed background
- high-scale typography
- minimal navigation
- sparse accent color
- flat components
- centered or poster-like hero composition
- large vertical rhythm

Design recipe:

- Set page background to `#000000`.
- Set primary text to `#ffffff`.
- Pick one accent, ideally red, blue, lime, or orange.
- Use the accent only for link hover, small labels, one marker line, one badge, or one CTA state.
- Use 0px radius for buttons and cards unless the brand has a deliberate geometric exception.
- Let sections breathe with 72px to 120px vertical separation.

Failure mode:

- The design becomes an empty black page with white text. Correct it by increasing typographic character, adding one strong layout gesture, and making the hero visual more specific.

### 3.2 Monochrome Blueprint

Inspired by Holographik.

This archetype is strict, industrial, and reduced. It uses black, white, and a single gray. Instead of relying on color, it builds identity from spacing, borders, radius, and type consistency.

Best for:

- design studios
- architecture sites
- experimental agencies
- product labs
- high-end technical services
- interaction studios

Core traits:

- black-white inversion
- 1px borders
- huge whitespace
- tight typography
- one font weight
- repeatable geometry
- unexpected radius signature

Design recipe:

- Use `#000000`, `#ffffff`, and one gray such as `#aaaaaa`.
- Use one font, one weight, and tight tracking.
- Choose one geometric signature: 0px sharp edge, 160px pill, square modules, circular buttons, or hairline panels.
- Use 120px to 220px gaps between major sections.
- Keep navigation bare.
- Avoid shadows.

Failure mode:

- The result looks unfinished. Correct it by adding a clear structural device: grid lines, huge section gaps, repeated pill modules, numbered blocks, or an architectural footer.

### 3.3 Cinematic Fashion Contrast

Inspired by HARDCLO.

This archetype combines stark UI with large imagery. The interface stays mostly achromatic while photography carries sensuality, product detail, and atmosphere.

Best for:

- fashion
- luxury goods
- editorial commerce
- fragrance
- jewelry
- galleries
- premium lifestyle

Core traits:

- split screen compositions
- black editorial canvas
- white micro-navigation
- large serif or high-character display type
- tiny functional sans labels
- imagery as the only major color field
- underline links instead of filled buttons

Design recipe:

- Use black UI panels beside full-height imagery.
- Pair a large serif headline with very small sans metadata.
- Keep interaction minimal: underlined text links, compact menus, simple product controls.
- Use 0px radius.
- Avoid bright UI accents unless the product itself owns the color.
- Let the image crop feel cinematic, not stock-like.

Failure mode:

- The site becomes inaccessible or overly fashion-vague. Correct it by adding clear product names, prices, sizes, filters, and checkout actions on calmer surfaces.

### 3.4 Light Stark Blueprint

Inspired by HyperAktiv.

This archetype proves high contrast can be light. It uses white backgrounds, black type, square controls, and one electric action color.

Best for:

- startups
- studios
- civic or institutional sites
- events
- productized services
- no-nonsense landing pages

Core traits:

- white canvas
- black typography
- one chromatic CTA
- square inputs and buttons
- strong display headings
- minimal cards
- forms that look functional rather than decorative

Design recipe:

- Use `#ffffff` as the default surface.
- Use `#000000` for headings, body, borders, and dividers.
- Pick one action color, often `#0000ff`.
- Keep components square: 0px radius for most buttons and inputs.
- Use 1px black or gray borders.
- Reserve rounded geometry for one special affordance, such as a floating feedback button.

Failure mode:

- The page feels like default HTML. Correct it with a stronger display type, larger hierarchy jumps, stricter spacing, and a more decisive CTA color.

### 3.5 Data Console Contrast

Inspired by Hugging Face.

This archetype supports density. It uses high contrast for readable data, cards, code, tags, controls, and information architecture.

Best for:

- AI products
- developer tools
- documentation hubs
- data platforms
- marketplaces
- model directories
- technical SaaS

Core traits:

- light background
- black primary text
- gray structural surfaces
- blue links
- semantic badges
- small radii
- subtle shadows
- compact repeated cards
- monospace for technical labels

Design recipe:

- Use white as primary page background.
- Use black for headings and high-value text.
- Use gray surfaces for grouping.
- Use blue only for links and true interactive states.
- Use 8px radius for cards, inputs, and buttons.
- Use monospace sparingly for code or technical metadata.
- Allow small semantic colors, but keep them contained inside badges, icons, tags, and status marks.

Failure mode:

- The interface becomes visually busy. Correct it by reducing accent color count, increasing group boundaries, and making list density more consistent.

## 4. Color System

### 4.1 Master Palette Families

High contrast palettes should be small. Most successful systems use one of these shapes.

#### Shape A: Absolute Monochrome

Use:

- `#000000`
- `#ffffff`

Optional:

- `#aaaaaa`
- `#e5e5e5`
- `#222222`

Best for:

- stark brand identity
- editorial impact
- portfolio drama
- concept sites

Rules:

- Do not add color because a section feels empty.
- Use spacing, scale, and inversion instead.
- If adding gray, assign it a specific role: divider, secondary text, inactive, disabled, or background.

#### Shape B: Monochrome Plus One Accent

Use:

- black
- white
- one gray
- one accent

Good accents:

- process red `#ee1c5b`
- electric blue `#0000ff`
- azure link `#155dfc`
- signal yellow `#ffd400`
- acidic green `#b6ff00`
- warning orange `#ff6900`

Best for:

- launches
- studios
- campaigns
- action-oriented landing pages

Rules:

- The accent must have a job.
- Never use the accent for paragraphs.
- Never use the accent on every card.
- Repeat the accent at least three times if it is part of identity: hero marker, CTA state, footer detail.

#### Shape C: Light Console

Use:

- canvas white
- near-black text
- gray border
- gray surface
- blue link
- small semantic accents

Best for:

- dashboards
- model directories
- docs
- marketplaces
- developer tools

Rules:

- Keep saturated colors small.
- Use gray to create information structure.
- Use black for primary text only; softer grays can support metadata.
- Keep link color stable and conventional.

#### Shape D: Cinematic Image Contrast

Use:

- black UI
- white text
- image color
- optional transparent overlays

Best for:

- luxury
- fashion
- music
- film
- products with strong photography

Rules:

- Do not compete with imagery using bright UI color.
- Use image crops as the color story.
- If overlaying text on images, test actual contrast at the crop.
- Keep navigation and metadata crisp.

| Token | Default | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |

| `--hc-carbon` | `#101828` | dark technical panel |

### 4.3 Accent Governance

High contrast design becomes cheap when every accent is loud. Govern accent use through allocation.

Use accent for:

- one primary CTA
- one active nav item
- one hero marker
- one data status
- one progress mark
- one editorial underline
- one price or availability indicator

Avoid accent for:

- every icon
- every card border
- body copy
- backgrounds behind long text
- decorative blobs
- hover states that compete with active states
- multiple unrelated semantic meanings

### 4.4 Background Pairings

| Background | Primary Text | Secondary Text | Border | CTA |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `#000000` | `#ffffff` | `#aaaaaa` | `#ffffff` at 1px or `#333333` | white, red, or blue |
| `#ffffff` | `#000000` | `#333333` or `#4a5565` | `#000000` or `#e5e7eb` | black or electric blue |
| `#101828` | `#ffffff` | `#d0d5dd` | `#344054` | blue or white |
| `#f3f4f6` | `#000000` | `#4a5565` | `#e5e7eb` | black or blue |
| image dark crop | `#ffffff` | `#f3f4f6` | transparent or white | white outline |

### 4.5 Anti-Mush Rules

- Do not use low-contrast gray text on black unless it is truly metadata.
- Do not use transparent white overlays that make the black canvas milky.
- Do not use gradients when a flat field would be stronger.
- Do not use tinted blacks from multiple hue families. Pick one dark base.
- Do not use dark blue as a generic black substitute unless the brand is technical.
- Do not let the accent become the surface color for every section.

## 5. Typography

### 5.1 High Contrast Type Jobs

High contrast typography needs clear jobs.

| Job | Recommended Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Hero display | huge, direct, high-character font, tight line-height |
| Functional nav | small sans, uppercase or compact casing, strong contrast |
| Body copy | readable sans or serif, moderate line-height |
| Metadata | small size, gray or inverse, disciplined spacing |
| Technical label | monospace or compact sans |
| Editorial quote | oversized serif or high-weight sans |
| CTA text | medium weight, short, no ambiguity |

### 5.2 Font Pairing Recipes

#### Pairing A: Custom Sans Only

Use one distinctive sans for all text.

Inspired by Colab and Holographik.

Best when:

- the brand font has enough character
- the layout is sparse
- the identity should feel designed but not decorative

Settings:

- hero: 64px to 112px
- section heading: 36px to 64px
- body: 16px to 20px
- nav: 12px to 15px
- weights: 400, 700, 900 or a single 400 if industrial
- letter spacing: 0em to 0.05em for character fonts; -0.02em for industrial tight systems

#### Pairing B: Serif Drama Plus Micro Sans

Use large serif for identity and tiny sans for function.

Inspired by HARDCLO.

Best when:

- the site is fashion, editorial, cultural, or luxury
- imagery is strong
- the UI needs to feel like a magazine or gallery

Settings:

- hero serif: 72px to 160px
- serif line-height: 0.9 to 1.05
- micro nav: 10px to 12px
- product labels: 11px to 14px
- letter spacing on micro text: -0.5px to 0.02em

#### Pairing C: Brutal Display Plus Clean Body

Use a loud display sans for headings and a calm body sans for clarity.

Inspired by HyperAktiv.

Best when:

- the brand is assertive
- the content includes forms or service explanations
- the page needs both impact and usability

Settings:

- display heading: 56px to 96px
- body: 16px to 20px
- body line-height: 1.35 to 1.55
- button weight: 600 or 700
- form labels: 14px to 16px

#### Pairing D: Console Sans Plus Mono

Use a legible sans for most content and monospace for technical signal.

Inspired by Hugging Face.

Best when:

- content is dense
- technical metadata matters
- the UI includes code, models, datasets, APIs, or docs

Settings:

- product heading: 40px to 72px
- card title: 16px to 20px
- metadata: 12px to 14px
- mono labels: 12px to 15px
- table text: 13px to 15px

### 5.3 Type Scale

Use these as starting values.

| Level | Dark Poster | Light Blueprint | Data Console | Fashion |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| hero | 72-120px | 72-96px | 48-72px | 96-160px |
| h1 | 56-80px | 56-80px | 40-60px | 72-112px |
| h2 | 36-56px | 36-48px | 30-40px | 44-64px |
| h3 | 24-32px | 24-32px | 20-24px | 24-32px |
| body | 16-20px | 16-20px | 14-16px | 14-18px |
| metadata | 11-13px | 12-14px | 12-13px | 10-12px |

### 5.4 Line Height Rules

- Hero display can use 0.88 to 1.05.
- Editorial display can use 0.9 to 1.1.
- Body copy should not use display tightness.
- Technical cards need 1.35 to 1.55 for scanning.
- Tiny navigation needs enough vertical hit area even when text is small.

### 5.5 Letter Spacing Rules

- Do not add negative tracking to body copy.
- Use tight tracking only for display, industrial headings, or compact nav.
- Use positive tracking for uppercase micro labels.
- If the font has strong character, avoid over-adjusting it.
- Never use letter spacing as a substitute for hierarchy.

## 6. Layout Systems

### 6.1 Layout Modes

#### Full-Bleed Poster

Use for:

- high-impact landing pages
- cultural sites
- campaigns
- creative studios

Structure:

1. minimal top nav
2. large centered or left-aligned hero type
3. one hero image or abstract field
4. generous vertical section breaks
5. sparse CTA
6. footer as final black-white composition

Measurements:

- section padding: 80px to 160px
- content max width: none or 1440px
- hero height: 70vh to 100vh
- nav height: 56px to 88px

#### Split Cinematic

Use for:

- fashion
- product imagery
- galleries
- premium objects

Structure:

1. left or right UI pane
2. full-height image pane
3. compact product information
4. underlined text actions
5. no decorative card framing

Measurements:

- split ratio: 40/60, 50/50, or 35/65
- image height: viewport or fixed aspect ratio
- text padding: 32px to 72px
- nav text: 10px to 14px

#### Light Stark Grid

Use for:

- service sites
- institutional pages
- practical landing pages

Structure:

1. white hero
2. black heading
3. square CTA
4. grid of text modules
5. forms with strong borders
6. one action color

Measurements:

- page padding: 24px to 64px
- grid gap: 16px to 32px
- button padding: 9px 15px minimum, often larger
- section gap: 48px to 96px

#### Dense Console

Use for:

- dashboards
- directories
- developer tools
- data platforms

Structure:

1. contained max-width shell
2. clear top navigation or sidebar
3. search/filter surface
4. repeated cards or rows
5. tags and metadata
6. precise empty/loading/error states

Measurements:

- max width: 1200px to 1440px
- card padding: 12px to 20px
- element gap: 8px to 16px
- card radius: 6px to 8px
- border: 1px gray

### 6.2 Section Rhythm

High contrast needs rhythm because the palette is reduced. Use spacing as a design element.

| Feeling | Section Gap | Element Gap | Internal Padding |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| theatrical | 80-160px | 10-24px | 24-64px |
| blueprint | 120-220px | 10-20px | 24-48px |
| fashion | 40-120px | 8-16px | 32-80px |
| light stark | 48-96px | 10-24px | 16-40px |
| console | 32-72px | 8-16px | 12-24px |

### 6.3 Grid Rules

- Use fewer columns than usual on poster-like pages.
- Use more columns only when the content needs comparison.
- Do not center every section if the page has dense information.
- Use full-bleed bands to create inversion moments.
- Let margins be intentionally wide on cinematic pages.
- Use the same left edge repeatedly for precision.
- Avoid floating cards inside floating cards.

### 6.4 Mobile Rules

- Preserve contrast, but reduce hero size.
- Do not let huge type create one-word-per-line fragments unless it is intentional.
- Stack split layouts with the image first only if product recognition matters; otherwise lead with the statement.
- Keep tiny fashion navigation accessible with larger hit areas.
- Dense console cards must become scannable rows or stacked modules.
- Avoid horizontal overflow from long display words.
- Keep CTA text short enough to fit.

## 7. Components

### 7.1 Button Families

#### Sharp Filled Button

Use for:

- dark poster CTA
- light stark CTA
- direct conversion action

Style:

- radius: 0px
- border: 1px solid currentColor or none
- padding: 12px 20px to 16px 32px
- text: medium or bold sans
- background: black, white, or one accent

States:

- hover: invert, underline, or accent shift
- focus: visible outline
- active: translate 1px or invert
- disabled: gray border and muted text

#### Ghost Outline Button

Use for:

- secondary actions on dark canvas
- hero secondary CTA
- editorial navigation

Style:

- transparent fill
- 1px white or black border
- matching text color
- radius depends on archetype
- no shadow

States:

- hover fill can invert
- focus outline must remain visible
- active should feel immediate, not soft

#### Extreme Pill Button

Use for:

- monochrome blueprint systems
- one signature interaction
- industrial identity

Style:

- radius: 999px or very high fixed radius
- border: 1px solid contrast color
- solid or inverse fill
- compact text

Rules:

- Do not use pills everywhere unless the entire system is built around them.
- Pair pills with strict typography so the result feels intentional.

#### Console Button

Use for:

- technical products
- dashboards
- docs
- data tools

Style:

- radius: 6px to 8px
- border: 1px gray
- subtle background change
- compact height
- icon optional

Rules:

- Do not make console buttons theatrical.
- Keep state colors semantic.

### 7.2 Cards

#### Naked Card

Use for:

- poster layouts
- editorial content
- image-led pages

Treatment:

- no border
- no shadow
- no background
- no radius
- content itself creates the block

#### Hairline Card

Use for:

- monochrome blueprint
- technical blocks
- comparison modules

Treatment:

- 1px border
- flat fill
- 0px or 8px radius
- no heavy shadow
- strong title

#### Data Console Card

Use for:

- repeated technical items
- directories
- model lists
- file lists

Treatment:

- white or gray surface
- 1px gray border
- 8px radius
- subtle shadow at most
- metadata rows
- tags and badges

#### Cinematic Product Panel

Use for:

- fashion/product
- galleries
- premium objects

Treatment:

- image dominant
- black or white info pane
- tiny labels
- underlined actions
- no decorative badges unless product needs status

### 7.3 Navigation

High contrast navigation should be quieter than the hero but clearer than decoration.

Patterns:

- minimal text nav on black
- black logo on white
- compact uppercase nav
- hamburger on poster pages
- plain link list for fashion sites
- console nav with search and tabs

Rules:

- Do not over-style nav just because the palette is simple.
- Use active state clearly.
- On dark backgrounds, test hover and focus states.
- Avoid tiny nav with tiny hit areas.
- Keep nav surface flat unless using console depth.

### 7.4 Forms

High contrast forms can look beautiful if they stay functional.

Dark form:

- black or carbon background
- white labels
- dark input fill or white input fill depending on context
- visible 1px border
- focus outline in accent color

Light form:

- white input
- black or gray border
- black label
- blue or black focus state
- square corners for stark systems

Rules:

- Placeholder text must be readable but not confused with input text.
- Error states need text, not just red border.
- Do not use low-contrast disabled states.
- Keep input height generous even if visual style is compact.

### 7.5 Badges And Tags

Use tags mostly in console or technical systems.

Good treatments:

- small gray pill
- square label with 1px border
- mono text tag
- semantic colored dot plus label
- inverse black-white badge

Avoid:

- rainbow tags without meaning
- badges larger than card titles
- rounded cute badges in a sharp editorial system
- color-only status communication

## 8. Imagery

### 8.1 Image Roles

High contrast imagery must have a role.

| Role | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Product evidence | proves the object, UI, or result |
| Cinematic mood | creates luxury or atmosphere |
| Editorial rhythm | breaks text-heavy composition |
| Technical preview | shows data, code, models, controls |
| Human contrast | adds warmth to a strict system |

### 8.2 Dark Canvas Imagery

Use:

- sharp crops
- strong silhouettes
- high-contrast photos
- full-height panels
- black overlays only when needed for text

Avoid:

- washed-out stock imagery
- low-contrast dark-on-dark photos
- decorative abstract gradients
- image filters that hide product detail

### 8.3 Light Stark Imagery

Use:

- clean product screenshots
- bold diagrams
- simple black-white illustrations
- direct portraits
- sharp documentary photos

Avoid:

- soft pastel image treatments
- decorative cutouts without information
- unnecessary mockup frames

### 8.4 Technical Console Imagery

Use:

- actual UI screenshots
- code snippets
- model cards
- tables
- diagrams
- compact previews

Rules:

- UI evidence must be legible.
- Do not blur the screenshot for mood.
- Keep technical images aligned to the grid.
- Use captions when the screenshot contains dense information.

## 9. Motion And Interaction

High contrast motion should be crisp.

### 9.1 Good Motion

- instant inversion on hover
- underline draw
- 1px border reveal
- image crop shift
- panel snap
- card lift by 1px to 2px only in console systems
- command-menu focus change
- active tab marker movement
- scroll-tied poster reveal

### 9.2 Bad Motion

- soft bouncy easing on a severe black-white identity
- floating blobs
- slow fade on primary interactions
- excessive parallax on text-heavy pages
- confetti unless the brand intentionally shifts tone
- hover effects that reduce contrast

### 9.3 Easing

Use:

- `cubic-bezier(0.2, 0, 0, 1)` for crisp UI
- `linear` for marquee or technical scans
- `ease-out` for simple reveal
- `steps()` for terminal or data motifs

Avoid:

- elastic easing for serious systems
- long 800ms hover animations
- motion that delays comprehension

- expose active state clearly

Do not:

- horizontal padding at least 16px

- cute microcopy

## 15. Accessibility

High contrast is not automatically accessible. Pure black and white can be readable, but it can also create glare and fatigue.

### 15.1 Contrast Rules

- Test all foreground/background pairs.
- Use pure white on pure black for short impact text.
- For long text on black, consider `#f3f4f6` instead of `#ffffff`.
- For long text on white, consider `#111111` instead of `#000000`.
- Use gray metadata only when still readable.
- Do not rely on color alone for active/error/success states.

### 15.2 Focus Rules

Every interactive element needs a visible focus state.

Dark canvas:

- white outline offset
- accent outline
- inverse fill

Light canvas:

- black outline
- blue outline
- 2px focus ring

Console:

- blue focus ring
- maintain border contrast

### 15.3 Motion Rules

- Respect reduced motion.
- Avoid flashing inversion patterns.
- Keep hover transitions under 200ms unless revealing a large section.
- Avoid scroll hijacking.

### 15.4 Readability Rules

- Do not set body copy at 10px.
- Do not use compressed display type for paragraphs.
- Keep line length manageable.
- Preserve adequate line height.
- Do not put long text on high-noise images.

## 16. Implementation Checklist

### 16.1 Before Designing

- Pick the archetype.
- Decide whether the page is dark, light, split, or console.
- Pick one accent policy.
- Pick one typography pairing.
- Decide radius signature.
- Decide image role.
- Decide whether shadows are forbidden or allowed only in console cards.

### 16.2 During Design

- Check first viewport at mobile and desktop.
- Verify one primary action is obvious.
- Ensure accent appears intentionally.
- Keep repeated components consistent.
- Use spacing as structure.
- Avoid cards where a full-width section is stronger.
- Confirm interactive states are visible.

### 16.3 Before Shipping

- Run color contrast checks.
- Test keyboard navigation.
- Test mobile wrapping for display text.
- Verify no text is hidden by image crops.
- Check empty and error states.
- Confirm CTA hierarchy.
- Remove decorative effects that reduce contrast.
- Verify the design still feels specific without source imagery.

## 17. Anti-Patterns

### 17.1 Faux High Contrast

Symptoms:

- dark gray background
- gray text
- weak CTA
- generic sans
- random gradients
- no hierarchy

Fix:

- commit to black-white contrast
- increase type scale
- remove unnecessary colors
- create a strong first viewport
- define one accent role

### 17.2 Inaccessible Poster

Symptoms:

- huge text but poor mobile wrapping
- tiny nav links
- text on busy image
- no focus states
- all interactions are visual-only

Fix:

- constrain display line length
- increase hit areas
- move text off the image or add a real overlay
- add focus ring
- provide text labels

### 17.3 Generic Brutalism

Symptoms:

- black-white palette
- default system font
- boxes everywhere
- no content specificity
- arbitrary borders

Fix:

- choose a type personality
- use fewer boxes
- create a meaningful grid
- add content-driven imagery
- make the accent rule stricter

### 17.4 Console Confetti

Symptoms:

- too many badge colors
- dense cards with no hierarchy
- mixed shadows and borders
- every icon has a different color

Fix:

- reduce badge palette
- standardize metadata rows
- use one card style
- reserve color for state and action

### 17.5 Fashion Vague

Symptoms:

- beautiful image
- unclear product
- tiny unreadable text
- no obvious purchase path

Fix:

- add clear product name and price
- create a visible action
- improve text contrast
- add details below the hero

## 18. Decision Trees

### 18.1 Dark Or Light

Use dark if:

- the brand needs drama
- imagery is cinematic
- the first impression matters more than long reading
- the site is cultural, creative, or premium

Use light if:

- forms matter
- dense content matters
- trust and clarity are the priority
- users will read, compare, or search

Use split if:

- product imagery is essential
- there is a premium or fashion object
- the site needs both atmosphere and information

Use console if:

- users must scan many items
- metadata matters
- the product is technical
- links, filters, and tags are core

### 18.2 Sharp Or Rounded

Use 0px radius if:

- the identity is stark
- the page feels editorial
- the brand is severe or technical
- you want poster authority

Use huge pill radius if:

- the system is otherwise strict
- the pill is a signature
- links/buttons need tactile contrast

Use 8px radius if:

- the interface is dense
- repeated cards need comfort
- technical users need scanability

Avoid mixed radius unless:

- each radius has a clear job
- the contrast is intentional

### 18.3 Accent Or No Accent

Use no accent if:

- photography is the color
- the typography is strong enough
- the brand must feel severe

Use one accent if:

- conversion matters
- active states need clarity
- the page needs a recurring marker

Use multiple semantic accents only if:

- the interface has real statuses
- each color maps to a defined meaning
- the accents are small

## 20. High Contrast QA Rubric

Score 1 to 5.

| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Contrast | muddy | readable | instantly separated |
| Hierarchy | flat | mostly clear | unmistakable |
| Identity | generic | some character | memorable and specific |
| Restraint | chaotic | mostly controlled | strict and intentional |
| Accessibility | risky | acceptable | robust |
| Responsiveness | breaks | acceptable | polished |
| Interaction | unclear | basic | crisp and accessible |
| Content Fit | decorative | partially aligned | content and style reinforce each other |

Target scores:

- Poster/brand page: 32+.
- Fashion/product page: 34+ with strong product clarity.
- Technical console: 35+ with high accessibility.
- Stark service page: 33+ with strong conversion clarity.

## 21. Prompt Templates

### 21.1 Dark Poster Brand

Create a high-contrast dark poster-style brand page. Use a pure black canvas, white typography, one disciplined accent color, sharp buttons, minimal navigation, and huge expressive hierarchy. The page should feel like a confident theatrical poster, not a generic dark SaaS page. Use spacing, scale, and inversion as the main design tools. Keep accent color limited to CTA, hover, or small graphic punctuation.

### 21.2 Monochrome Blueprint

Create a strict monochrome high-contrast interface using black, white, and one gray. Build identity through typography, spacing, hairline borders, and a repeatable geometric signature. Avoid gradients, shadows, and decorative colors. The result should feel architectural, industrial, and precise.

### 21.3 Cinematic Product

Create a cinematic high-contrast product page with black UI, white editorial typography, and large product imagery as the only major color source. Pair a dramatic display typeface with compact functional labels. Use underlined links or sharp buttons. Make product details and purchase actions clear without reducing the premium editorial mood.

### 21.4 Light Stark Landing

Create a light high-contrast landing page using white background, black typography, square controls, strong section rules, and one electric action color. The result should feel direct, functional, and visually assertive. Keep forms crisp, hierarchy bold, and the accent color reserved for real actions.

### 21.5 Data Console

Create a high-contrast data console for a technical product. Use a bright white canvas, black headings, gray structural surfaces, blue links, small semantic badges, compact cards, and monospace labels only for technical metadata. The interface should be dense but readable, with consistent spacing and accessible states.

## 23. Last-Mile Corrections

If it feels too empty:

- increase headline specificity
- add one strong image or index structure
- introduce a hairline grid
- add a repeated geometric signature
- use inversion between sections

If it feels too harsh:

- soften long text from pure white to off-white
- use carbon instead of pure black for content-heavy zones
- increase line-height
- use gray surfaces for console areas
- reduce all-caps usage

If it feels too generic:

- replace default font
- define a radius rule
- make accent use stricter
- add a distinctive layout mode
- add content-specific imagery

If it feels inaccessible:

- increase body size
- improve focus states
- move text off images
- reduce low-opacity text
- add non-color state cues

If it feels too colorful:

- remove accents from decorative places
- keep color in CTA/status only
- turn cards back to monochrome
- let imagery carry color

- Long pages have rhythm through spacing or inversion.

## 26. Detailed Build Patterns

### 26.1 Poster Landing Page Pattern

Use this pattern when the page is mostly persuasion and identity.

Required sections:

1. `PosterHero`
2. `SignalProof`
3. `WorkOrFeatureBand`
4. `InvertedStatement`
5. `ProcessIndex`
6. `FinalCTA`

Section logic:

- The hero must create the visual signature.
- The proof section must make the signature credible.
- The feature band must show the product, not just describe it.
- The statement section can be typographic and dramatic.
- The process index should use hard rules, numbers, or a simple row system.
- The final CTA should repeat the primary contrast relationship.

Implementation notes:

- Do not create a floating card hero.
- Do not center all sections unless the whole page is intentionally poster-like.
- Keep the first CTA visible without needing a scroll.
- If the hero is all text, make the text distinctive enough to carry the page.

## 27. Microinteraction Recipes

### 27.1 Inversion Hover

Best for:

- sharp buttons
- row links
- monochrome cards
- project indexes

Behavior:

- background and text swap
- border remains visible
- transition under 160ms

CSS:

```css
.hc-invert {
  color: #ffffff;
  background: #000000;
  border: 1px solid #ffffff;
  transition: color 140ms ease-out, background 140ms ease-out, border-color 140ms ease-out;
}

.hc-invert:hover,
.hc-invert:focus-visible {
  color: #000000;
  background: #ffffff;
  border-color: #ffffff;
}
```

### 27.2 Hairline Row Reveal

Best for:

- portfolios
- indexes
- documentation lists
- technical directories

Behavior:

- row background changes slightly
- arrow appears or shifts
- title remains readable

CSS:

```css
.hc-row {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-columns: 1fr auto;
  gap: 24px;
  border-top: 1px solid currentColor;
  padding: 18px 0;
}

.hc-row__arrow {
  transform: translateX(-4px);
  opacity: 0;
  transition: transform 140ms ease-out, opacity 140ms ease-out;
}

.hc-row:hover .hc-row__arrow {
  transform: translateX(0);
  opacity: 1;
}
```

### 27.3 Console Focus Ring

Best for:

- search fields
- filters
- developer tools
- data-heavy pages

Behavior:

- blue outline appears
- border remains stable
- no layout shift

CSS:

```css
.hc-field {
  border: 1px solid #e5e7eb;
  border-radius: 8px;
  background: #ffffff;
  color: #000000;
  outline: none;
}

.hc-field:focus-visible {
  border-color: #155dfc;
  box-shadow: 0 0 0 3px rgba(21, 93, 252, 0.18);
}
```

### 27.4 Image Crop Shift

Best for:

- cinematic product panels
- editorial image grids
- portfolio previews

Behavior:

- image scale increases slightly
- crop shifts by 1 to 3 percent
- overlay text remains stable

Rules:

- Do not scale beyond 1.04.
- Keep duration around 300ms.
- Disable for reduced motion.
- Avoid if product detail becomes harder to inspect.

## 29. Section-by-Section Contrast Checks

## 30. Brand Calibration

### 30.1 If The Brand Is Creative

Emphasize:

- large type
- black canvas
- sparse copy
- project imagery
- one striking accent

Reduce:

- dense cards
- complex forms
- console-like gray panels

### 30.2 If The Brand Is Technical

Emphasize:

- search
- filters
- metadata
- cards
- monospace labels
- blue links

Reduce:

- over-large editorial type
- vague statements
- black backgrounds for long tasks

### 30.3 If The Brand Is Luxury

Emphasize:

- image scale
- serif drama
- micro labels
- black-white restraint
- slow but crisp transitions

Reduce:

- bright accents
- heavy outlines
- badge systems
- over-explained sections

### 30.4 If The Brand Is Civic Or Institutional

Emphasize:

- white background
- black typography
- clear forms
- strong rules
- accessible focus states

Reduce:

- theatrical darkness
- tiny text
- ambiguous navigation

### 30.5 If The Brand Is Startup SaaS

Emphasize:

- light stark layout
- one action color
- clear product screenshots
- direct copy
- conversion path

Reduce:

- empty black hero if product is hard to understand
- luxury vagueness
- random saturated decoration

## 31. Deep Anti-Slop Rules

1. Do not use black-white contrast as an excuse to skip composition.
2. Do not use generic Inter at default sizes unless the layout is doing something strong.
3. Do not put white 16px text across a full-width black paragraph block without checking reading comfort.
4. Do not let the accent color appear in places that are not actionable or meaningful.
5. Do not add shadows to a flat poster system.
6. Do not use blur backgrounds in a monochrome blueprint unless the brand explicitly needs glass.
7. Do not place every piece of content in a card.
8. Do not mix square, 8px, 24px, and pill radii randomly.
9. Do not make a data console black-on-black if users need to scan for hours.
10. Do not create fashion layouts where product details are unreadable.
11. Do not hide navigation behind ambiguous icon-only controls on content-heavy pages.
12. Do not use low-opacity white as a lazy secondary text color.
13. Do not use gradients as the main high-contrast move.
14. Do not make all text uppercase.
15. Do not use huge type on mobile without testing the longest word.
16. Do not use multiple bright accents for brand energy.
17. Do not make hover states lower contrast than default states.
18. Do not rely on underlines alone if link color and context are unclear.
19. Do not use thin font weights on pure black hero sections unless the font is built for it.
20. Do not treat screenshots as decorative; technical screenshots must be readable.

## 33. Final High Contrast Heuristic

A high contrast interface is working when:

- the user knows where to look first
- the palette feels chosen, not missing
- the accent feels earned
- typography carries identity
- spacing feels structural
- components have a consistent geometry
- imagery has a clear job
- interactions become clearer on hover/focus
- mobile keeps the same attitude
- the design would still be recognizable in grayscale

If one of those points fails, fix the underlying system rather than adding decoration.
